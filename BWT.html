AQA A-Level Geography (7037): Interactive Guide

Assessment Overview

Component 1: Physical Geography

Weighting: 40% of A-Level Format: Written Exam Duration: 2 hrs 30 mins Marks: 120
Content Breakdown (Sections & Marks)
  • Section A: Water and Carbon Cycles (36 marks) Key concepts: Systems (inputs, outputs, stores, flows, feedback), global distribution, drainage basins, carbon budget, climate links.
  • Section B: Choose ONE (36 marks):
    • Hot Desert Systems and Landscapes Key concepts: Aridity index, energy/sediment budgets, aeolian/fluvial processes, desertification.
    • Coastal Systems and Landscapes Key concepts: Energy/sediment budgets, erosion/deposition processes, sea level change (e.g., last 10,000 years), management.
    • Glacial Systems and Landscapes Key concepts: Budgets, cold environments, glacial/fluvioglacial/periglacial processes, Pleistocene impacts.
  • Section C: Choose ONE (48 marks):
    • Hazards Key concepts: Perception, responses (Park Model, Hazard Management Cycle), plate tectonics, specific hazard processes (volcanic, seismic, storm, fire).
    • Ecosystems under Stress Key concepts: Biodiversity, succession (climatic climax, plagioclimax), nutrient cycling, biome characteristics, sustainability.

Component 2: Human Geography

Weighting: 40% of A-Level Format: Written Exam Duration: 2 hrs 30 mins Marks: 120
Content Breakdown (Sections & Marks)
  • Section A: Global Systems and Global Governance (36 marks) Key concepts: Globalisation dimensions (flows, TNCs), interdependence, inequalities, global commons governance (esp. Antarctica), trade patterns.
  • Section B: Changing Places (36 marks) Key concepts: Place meaning (insider/outsider), representation, endogenous/exogenous factors, shifting flows, demographic/cultural/economic change.
  • Section C: Choose ONE (48 marks):
    • Contemporary Urban Environments Key concepts: Urbanisation trends (esp. post-1945), world/megacities, urban forms/processes (segregation, inequality, SUDs, UHI), sustainability (ecological footprint, liveability).
    • Population and the Environment Key concepts: Population dynamics (DTM), distribution/density, migration, food security, health/disease patterns, resources (Malthus/Boserup).
    • Resource Security Key concepts: Resource classification, peak resources, geopolitics, water/energy/mineral security, sustainability (EIA), supply/demand management.

Component 3: Geography Fieldwork Investigation (NEA)

Weighting: 20% of A-Level Format: Independent Investigation Word Count: 3,000 - 4,000 Marks: 60
Key Requirements
  • Independent question/issue defined by student, linked to specification.
  • Must include primary field data.
  • Independent analysis, interpretation, evaluation, write-up.
  • Requires understanding of methods, ethics, limitations.

Geographical Skills Checklist

Competence needed in qualitative and quantitative skills, including:

  • Cartographic Skills
  • Graphical Skills
  • Statistical Skills (Descriptive & Inferential - e.g., Spearman's Rank, Chi-Square)
  • ICT Skills (GIS, databases, presentation)
  • Use & Annotation of Visuals
  • Literacy & Numeracy Application
  • Qualitative Data Collection & Analysis
  • Understanding 'Big Data' & Crowd-Sourcing
  • Use of Remotely Sensed Data
  • Ethical Considerations
  • Evaluation of Data/Methods

Essential A-Level Geography Case Studies

The following list details key case studies that are highly recommended or explicitly required for a comprehensive understanding of the AQA A-Level Geography (7037) specification. Expand each topic to see relevant examples.

Component 1: Physical Geography

Water and Carbon Cycles
  • Amazon Rainforest: (Tropical Rainforest) For cycles, impacts of deforestation, climate change links.
  • Indonesian Rainforest: (Tropical Rainforest) For cycles, impacts of deforestation (e.g., palm oil), carbon storage.
  • River Wye, Wales: (River Catchment) Detailed study of catchment processes, landforms, and human impacts.
  • River Exe, Devon: (River Catchment) Characteristics, flood risk, and management strategies (e.g., Exe Estuary Management Partnership).
  • Boscastle Floods (2004): (River Catchment) Case study of a flash flood event, causes, impacts, and responses.
Hazards
  • Volcanic Hazards:
    • Mt Etna, Italy: Frequent eruptions, monitoring, management in a MEDC.
    • Mt Merapi, Java, Indonesia: Explosive eruptions, impacts in a LEDC/EDC, evacuation strategies.
    • Eyjafjallajökull, Iceland (2010): Ash cloud impacts on aviation, global interconnectedness.
    • Montserrat (Soufrière Hills Volcano): Long-term eruption, socio-economic impacts, resettlement.
  • Seismic Hazards:
    • Haiti Earthquake (2010): Devastating impacts in a very low-income country, challenges of aid and recovery.
    • Tohoku, Japan Earthquake and Tsunami (2011): High-magnitude event in a highly developed country, preparedness, complex impacts.
    • Indonesian Tsunami (Boxing Day 2004): Causes, widespread regional impacts, international response.
  • Storm Hazards:
    • Cyclone Nargis, Myanmar (2008): Tropical storm impacts in a low-income country with political complexities.
    • Hurricane Katrina, New Orleans, USA (2005): Tropical storm impacts in a developed country, issues of social vulnerability.
  • Wildfire Hazards:
    • The Alberta Wildfires, Canada (e.g., Fort McMurray 2016): Causes, impacts on environment and economy, management.
    • Los Angeles/California Wildfires (various years): Human-environment interactions, climate factors, urban-wildland interface.
  • Multi-Hazardous Environment: Example like The Philippines or Tohoku, Japan (already covered by specific hazards).
  • Local Scale Hazard (UK): A UK-based hazard event illustrating its physical nature and community response (e.g., Boscastle Floods 2004, Somerset Levels Floods 2013-14).
Coastal Systems and Landscapes
  • Holderness Coast, UK: (Coastal Environment) Rapid erosion, landforms (e.g., Flamborough Head, Spurn Head), coastal management strategies and conflicts.
  • Swanage, Dorset, UK: (Coastal Environment) Classic coastal landforms (e.g., headlands, bays, stacks, stumps like Old Harry Rocks).
  • Plymouth, UK: (Coastal Environment) Coastal management strategies in an urbanised coastal setting.
  • Odisha, India: (Contrasting Coastal Landscape) Coastal risks (e.g., cyclones, erosion), opportunities (e.g., tourism, fishing), and management strategies (e.g., ICZM, mangrove restoration).

Component 2: Human Geography

Global Systems and Global Governance
  • Antarctica: Required study as a global common. Focus on threats (climate change, tourism, resource exploitation) and governance (Antarctic Treaty System, role of UNEP, IWC, NGOs).
  • TNC Study: Coca-Cola or Apple: Detailed study of one TNC, its global operations, spatial organisation, impacts on host/origin countries, and ethical considerations.
  • Global Trade in a Commodity: Bananas: Study the global trade of bananas, including supply chains, role of TNCs, fair trade schemes, and environmental/social issues.
  • Global Governance Institutions: Understanding the role and effectiveness of organisations like the UN and its agencies (e.g., WHO, UNEP) in managing global issues.
Changing Places
  • Understanding Place: Meaning & Representation:
    • Ground Zero, New York City: Exploring how places acquire deep symbolic meaning and are represented post-event.
    • Stonehenge/Glastonbury, UK: Examining historical, cultural, and spiritual meanings attached to places and their varied representations.
  • Economic Change & Social Inequality in Places:
    • Detroit, USA: Case study of deindustrialisation, economic decline, population loss, and subsequent efforts towards regeneration and diversification, highlighting resulting social inequalities.
    • Plymouth, UK (Economic Focus): Analyzing historical economic shifts (e.g., naval decline, manufacturing changes) and current economic challenges, regeneration projects, and associated inequalities within the city.
  • Place Regeneration & Rebranding Strategies:
    • Medellin, Colombia: Transformation from a city known for high crime rates to an innovative urban hub through social urbanism, infrastructure projects, and community engagement.
    • Llandudno, UK: Rebranding efforts of a traditional coastal resort to attract new tourism demographics and sustain its economy.
    • London Olympics (2012 Legacy): Examining the regeneration of East London, focusing on infrastructure development, economic impacts, and social changes in the host boroughs.
  • Influence of External Forces & Agents of Change:
    • Bourneville, Birmingham, UK: The impact of Cadbury's philanthropic vision (an external agent) in creating a model village, shaping its character, and social fabric.
    • Devonport, Plymouth, UK: How national government policy (e.g., naval base investment/downsizing), and large-scale regeneration schemes have shaped the identity and socio-economic landscape of this specific locality.
Contemporary Urban Environments
  • Global Urbanisation & Megacity Dynamics:
    • Las Vegas, USA: Rapid urban growth in an arid environment, focusing on resource management (especially water) and unique economic drivers.
    • Los Angeles (LA), USA: Characteristics of a sprawling megacity, including urban form, transport challenges, social segregation, and environmental pressures.
    • Shanghai, China: Exemplar of rapid economic growth and urbanisation, leading to megacity status, significant infrastructure development, and challenges of scale.
    • Mumbai, India: A megacity illustrating extreme contrasts of wealth and poverty (e.g., Dharavi slum), challenges of housing, sanitation, and transport in a densely populated coastal setting.
    • Bengaluru (Bangalore), India: Rise as a global tech hub (Silicon Plateau), leading to rapid population growth, infrastructure strain, and changing urban landscape.
    • A Major City in Sub-Saharan Africa (e.g., Lagos, Nairobi, Kinshasa): Choose one to study rapid urbanisation, informal economy, informal settlements, infrastructure deficits, and governance challenges.
  • Urban Forms, Processes & Socio-Economic Issues:
    • Social Segregation & Inequality: Drawing examples from studied megacities (e.g., LA, Mumbai, South African cities) to illustrate patterns and causes of residential segregation and socio-economic disparities.
    • Post-Industrial Urban Challenges (e.g., Plymouth, UK): Examining the legacy of deindustrialisation, unemployment, dereliction, and the need for economic restructuring in cities like Plymouth.
  • Urban Regeneration & Redevelopment Strategies:
    • London Docklands, UK: Large-scale, market-led regeneration transforming a derelict industrial area into a new financial and residential hub.
    • Longbridge, Birmingham, UK: Regeneration of a former major car manufacturing site, focusing on mixed-use development and job creation.
    • Olympic Park Regeneration (London 2012): Specific focus on the redevelopment of the Lower Lea Valley for the Olympics and its ongoing legacy.
  • Sustainable Urban Development & Management:
    • Freiburg, Germany (Vauban District): A leading example of sustainable urban living, focusing on green building, renewable energy, sustainable transport, and community participation.
    • Sustainable Urban Drainage Systems (SUDs): Lamb Drove, Cambourne, UK: A case study of SUDs implementation to manage surface water runoff effectively.
    • River Restoration in an Urban Setting: River Skerne, Darlington, UK: Example of restoring a heavily modified urban river to improve environmental quality and amenity value.
    • Urban Waste Management Strategies: Comparing different approaches (e.g., recycling, incineration, landfill, composting) in a specific urban area (e.g., a chosen UK city or a contrasting international city).
    • Urban Heat Island (UHI) Mitigation: Strategies employed in cities (e.g., green roofs, reflective surfaces, urban greening) to reduce the UHI effect (use examples from major cities).
